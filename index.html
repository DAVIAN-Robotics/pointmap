Cameras Move. Actions Don't.

Robot demonstrations are collected with cameras at ever-different placements, yet the policy must always act in one fixed robot frame. The more the training viewpoints vary, the harder the policy finds it to map what it sees to how it should move.

34.5% → 24.9%
RGB-only success keeps falling as training-camera variation grows from none to high (RoboCasa H50).

The root cause is a frame mismatch. Observations arrive in the camera frame, while actions are defined in the robot frame, and every camera move rewrites the mapping between them.

3D in the Robot Frame, Shaped like an Image

We close this gap at the input. The observation we feed the policy should carry 3D geometry for manipulation, express it in the robot's own coordinate frame, and still fit the dense image interface of a pretrained VLA. Which 3D input does all three?

A robot-centric pointmap stores, at each pixel, the 3D coordinate of that pixel's scene point in the robot frame. The same physical point keeps the same value no matter where the camera is placed. We additionally re-center it at the current end-effector position.

Requirements: an RGB-D camera with calibrated intrinsics and camera-to-robot extrinsics (a one-time hand-eye calibration on the real robot), at both training and test time.

Four Controlled Studies, One Design

In controlled studies on RoboCasa H50, we hold the backbone and training recipe fixed and change only the 3D input. Click each takeaway for the evidence.

These studies use a π-style backbone initialized from raw PaliGemma (no robot pretraining), so absolute numbers are lower than the pretrained-VLA results below.

All variants below see the same scene, and the last two receive the same depth, intrinsics, and extrinsics. Pre-computing the transform into a pointmap is worth +3.1 points over leaving it for the policy to infer.

2Keep 3D in image form. Pointmaps outperform point clouds.▼
RQ2 · Image-form pointmap or point cloud?

Preserving the H × W grid lets pointmap tokens be added element-wise to spatially corresponding RGB tokens, reaching 34.7 SR, above a pretrained Point Transformer v3 point-cloud branch (32.8) and an MLP point-cloud branch (24.2). Concatenating pointmap tokens as a separate sequence instead of adding them drops performance to 30.7.

3Center the pointmap at the end effector, not the robot base.▼
RQ3 · Which coordinate origin?

In the robot-base frame, grasp targets are scattered across the workspace. In the EE-centered frame they collapse near (0, 0, 0) at grasp time, giving the policy a consistent local geometry. EE-centering lifts SR from 34.7 to 36.9, and under randomized evaluation viewpoints it degrades by only −0.3 vs. −2.0 for base centering.

4The benefit grows as training-time viewpoint variation grows.▼
RQ4 · Does the design survive more camera randomization?

We jitter RoboCasa's camera poses per demonstration, from No variation to High (the same three levels as the graph at the top of the page, shown below). Where the RGB-only policy kept falling, the pointmap policy barely moves, 37.6 → 35.8, and its margin widens across the levels, +3.1 → +9.0 → +10.9.

Pointmaps Lift Both VLA Backbones

On RoboCasa H50 (24 tasks, 50 demos each), pointmaps lift two pretrained VLAs of different scales and action experts, +7.6 on π0.5 and +4.2 on SmolVLA. π0.5 + pointmap reaches 62.9 average success, ahead of every camera-aware, 3D-augmented, and point-cloud baseline. All baselines except FP3 share the π0.5 backbone, so the comparison isolates how spatial information is supplied.

On a Real Robot, the Gap Widens on an Unseen Camera

We collect demonstrations on a Franka Research 3 while physically repositioning the external camera across three placements (180 demos over 4 tasks), then evaluate at a seen placement and at a held-out (unseen) placement. The pointmap's margin over RGB-only π0.5 widens from +5.0 at the seen placement to +11.7 at the unseen one.
