See like a Robot: Robot-Centric Pointmaps for Vision-Language-Action Models

Byungkun Lee1*, Dongyoon Hwang1*, Minho Park1, Hojoon Lee2, Dongjin Kim1, Jaegul Choo1

1KAIST 2Holiday Robotics

*Equal contribution

We introduce robot-centric pointmaps, which express per-pixel 3D geometry in the robot frame while preserving the dense image grid used by pretrained VLAs.

Cameras Move. Actions Don't.

A VLA observes the scene from the camera frame but predicts actions in the robot frame. When the camera viewpoint changes, the visual observation changes even though the required robot action is defined in the same robot frame. The policy must therefore handle a different observation-to-action relationship across viewpoints.

With a fixed camera, this relationship remains consistent across the dataset. As training demonstrations are collected from increasingly diverse viewpoints, however, learning this relationship becomes more difficult.

34.5% → 24.9%
RGB-only success keeps falling as training-time viewpoint variation grows from none to high (RoboCasa H50).

3D in the Robot Frame, Shaped like an RGB Image

We close this gap at the input. A robot-centric pointmap combines two properties:

  • Robot-frame geometry. Each pixel stores a 3D coordinate in the frame where robot actions are defined.
  • Image-form structure. The dense H × W grid is preserved, allowing the pointmap to use the image-based visual pathway of a pretrained VLA.

A robot-centric pointmap stores robot-frame XYZ coordinates at the corresponding image pixels, preserving the same dense H × W grid as RGB.

Pointmaps Remain Stable as Training-Time Viewpoint Variation Increases

We now revisit the training-time viewpoint variation study with pointmaps. Both policies share the same backbone and training recipe, and camera poses are jittered per demonstration from No variation to High (shown below). As viewpoint variation across training demonstrations increases, RGB-only performance drops from 34.5% to 24.9%, whereas RGB + Pointmap decreases only from 37.6% to 35.8%.

37.6% → 35.8%
The pointmap margin over RGB-only grows from +3.1 to +10.9 as training-time viewpoint variation increases.

Three Controlled Studies, One Design

Why does the design look the way it does? Each choice is backed by its own controlled ablation on RoboCasa H50. The backbone and training recipe stay fixed, and only the 3D input changes.

For these controlled studies, we use a model without robot pretraining to isolate the effect of each input design. Its success rates are therefore lower than those of the pretrained VLAs evaluated later.

1Pre-compute Robot-Frame Geometry▼

Plücker rays describe the camera geometry, and depth provides per-pixel distance. RGB + Plücker + Depth therefore contains the information needed to obtain robot-frame geometry, but leaves it for the policy to infer. Providing robot-frame geometry directly as a pointmap improves success from 31.6% to 34.7%.

On a Real Robot, the Gap Widens at an Unseen Camera Placement

We collect demonstrations on a Franka Research 3 while physically repositioning the external camera across three placements (180 demos over 4 tasks), then evaluate at a seen placement and at a held-out (unseen) placement. The pointmap advantage grows from +5.0 points at the seen camera placement to +11.7 points at the unseen placement.
